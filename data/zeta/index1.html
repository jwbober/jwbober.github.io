Computations of the Riemann zeta function

Here are some pictures of and information about Z(t) and S(t) for some interesting values of t. The Z function is the zeta function on the critical line, rotated so that it is real, so

Z(t) = e^(i Arg(zeta(1/2 + it)) zeta(1/2 + it).

S(t) is the argument of zeta(1/2 + it), properly interpreted. In some way, it measures irregularity in the distribution of the zeros of the zeta function.

These are from computations run by Ghaith Hiary and myself, based on the algorithm described in Ghaith's paper (also available at the arXiv). If you would like to see our code, it is publicly visible, but it is not really released in a usable form. These computations ran/are running mostly on a cluster of machines named riemann. In the past, we also used the sage cluster at the University of Washington.

If your web browser window is big enough, in the top right of each section below you will see a plot of Z(t), in the bottom left you will see S(t), and in the bottom right you will see a zoomed in plot of Z(t). Things are sized roughly so that this looks good on my 1080p monitor. In all of the images, 0 corresponds to the value of t listed above the image, and in the image filename. Little bits of the right and left of the plots are left off because the band limited interpolation that we use requires a large enough interval of data to interpolate on, and at the edges we don't have that.

You can click on any image for a bigger version. Also, you can look at a list of all of the images: Z(t) or S(t).
